Wake Me Up At...

Because taking a nap is free, but skipping work isn't.

  • Strategies:

  • Product design

  • Motion graphics

  • Digital illustration

  • Laser engraving

  • Timeline:

  • Nov - Dec 2019

  • Tools used:

  • Adobe After Effects

  • Adobe Illustrator

  • Dremel LC40 Laser Cutter

Wake Me Up At... is a project I created while at NYU, inspired by consumerism and the 9-5 commute.

The Inpiration

I wanted to create a short advertisement that markets something completely free and intangible. My goal was to capitalize a simple feature that every human has access to, and use typical elements seen in video ads to exploit it. I chose naps as my subject because it is one of the most universally accessible tasks.

The Physical Product

From there, I started thinking about ways to create a physical object that could actually be marketable. My challenge was deciding what I could make based off of the immaterial concept of naps.

I was really attached by one of my favorite illustrations from the ad: the girl sleeping on the subway. I started thinking about the commuter life, and the experience of dozing off on public transportation that many of us have shared.

Illustration drawn for my digital ad.

This lead to an idea of creating a product that could help people nap on subways with no consequences. After all, taking a nap may be free, but skipping work isn't.

My prototype is a silk sleeping mask, with different subway stations printed onto it to inform bystanders of when to wake the user up for their stop. I chose the sleeping mask since it's an iconic symbol of sleeping, specifically a silk mask to symbolize luxury which juxtaposes against the dirty subway.

The vector files, pre-engraving

I was also inspired by other sleeping masks with catchy quotes on them, and chose a script font to allude to the soft and cursive type usually associated with sleeping and dreaming.

This digitally-created design was printed on the masks using a Dremel LC40 Laser Cutter. A lot of experimenting was required beforehand, since I was unsure which settings would work best on the silk material without burning through it.

The finished piece. No burns here!

What's next?

My next goal is to take these masks out into the real world, and conduct some social experiments to see if New Yorkers are actually willing to wake up a napping stranger wearing a sleep mask engraved with instructions.

It would also be fun to make more of these masks with even more subway stops, perhaps embroidered on instead of engraved (laser engraved silk is extremely fragile and also smells a little odd).

Expect to see some documentation of these future steps very soon!
